National Overview: A Thriving Ecosystem

Current State & Economic Impact

New Zealand's technology startup ecosystem demonstrates robust health with approximately 2,400 active startups as of 2025. The sector attracted over NZD 2 billion in investment during 2024 and catalyzed the creation of over 40,000 new jobs in 2023. [1]

The tech sector's contribution to GDP stands at NZD 23 billion, surpassing many traditional industries. Each new tech job creates an additional 4.8 jobs elsewhere in the economy, demonstrating significant multiplier effects. [1]

Looking forward, NZD 236 million is being invested in 72 groundbreaking projects over the next five years, focusing on quantum computing, environmental technology, and digital infrastructure. [1]

Critical Gaps & Barriers

Underinvestment in Research & Development

New Zealand invests approximately 1.47% of GDP in R&D, substantially below the OECD average of 2.7%. Leading innovative nations like Germany, Sweden, and South Korea invest over 3% of GDP. [26]

Impact on Productivity

Treasury report highlights low R&D investment as possible reason for poor productivity performance. [27]

Access to Capital: The Series A/B Gap

While pre-seed funding reached over NZD 100 million in 2025, access to sufficient capital for Series A and B funding rounds remains challenging, creating a "valley of death" for scaling ventures. [8]

Systemic Challenges

  • • Conservative banking sector approach to risk
  • • Relatively small domestic market size
  • • Difficulty attracting larger scaling investments
  • • Limited risk-taking entrepreneurship culture
[28]

SME Cash Flow Reality

Xero research shows average NZ SME experiences negative cash flow for 4 months per year, with 17% struggling for over 6 months. [29]

Government Response

NZGCP's Elevate fund aims to address gap by investing NZ$196M into eight Kiwi VCs, targeting Series A and B funding gaps. [30]

Talent Shortage: Skills Gap and Brain Drain

Persistent shortage of skilled talent in specialized tech roles, exacerbated by "brain drain" where skilled professionals move overseas, often to Australia for higher wages and broader opportunities. [29]

Government Initiatives

  • • Digital Technologies ITP: "enhancing skills and talent pipeline"
  • • Draft RSI strategy: making NZ talent magnet
  • • Skilled workforce target: 70% (from current 67%)

Strategic Outlook: Navigating the Future

Current Position

New Zealand's innovation ecosystem in 2025 demonstrates significant strength in AI, Agritech, SaaS, Fintech, and Healthtech sectors, supported by growing public and private investment networks. The ecosystem has proven its economic contribution through 40,000+ new jobs in 2023 and NZD 2B+ invested in 2024.

Key Strengths

  • • Strong sector specialization in high-growth areas
  • • Increasing venture capital participation (58% of deals)
  • • High AI adoption rate (67% of companies)
  • • Significant multiplier effect (4.8 jobs per tech job)

Critical Challenges

However, persistent barriers threaten long-term competitiveness: R&D underinvestment at 1.47% of GDP (vs OECD 2.7%), Series A/B funding gaps hindering scale-up, and talent shortages exacerbated by brain drain.

Strategic Response

  • • National AI Strategy 2025 provides coordinated framework
  • • City and Regional Deals enable place-based innovation
  • • Continued Elevate Fund investment in domestic VCs
  • • Science system restructuring for better R&D alignment

Path Forward

By proactively addressing R&D investment gaps, funding access challenges, and talent shortages while capitalizing on emerging opportunities through the National AI Strategy and Regional Deals, New Zealand can position innovation as a primary driver of economic prosperity and global competitiveness.

Success depends on fostering risk-embracing culture, strategic investment in research and talent, and building robust pathways for scaling innovative ventures globally.
